Music

A big part of the Harlem Renaissance was the music scene. Mass production of music allowed African American music to be heard around the world. Jazz and the Blues had brought creative expression of oppression and everyday life of black people to the masses. It granted a platform to make money and experiment and redefine and explore identity. Prohibition also made clubs popular to drink and have a good time. Artists would make money while entertaining.

Dozens of jazz clubs would surround Harlem including the infamous Apollo Theater. Louis Armstrong, the legendary trumpeter played many times at these establishments. One of the most popular clubs in Harlem was the Cotton Club. Cab Calloway and Duke Ellington, two talented band leaders and musicians would be known associated acts there. They gained national exposure and the nightlife in Harlem grew. Vaudeville grew out of Minstrel Theater and both Ma Rainey and her protégé, Bessie Smith, would be famous for performing at these legendary spots. They both grew the blues and jazz scene in theater along with many other prominent artists and dancers.

Out of these clubs, music evolved – ragtime, stride jazz, scatting, bebop and many other genres and techniques. This musical platform allowed black artists to express and experiment. Ma Rainey and Bessie Smith were both known to subvert gender expression and sexuality through fashion and their musical lyrics. The LGTBQ community and freedom of expression grew during the Harlem Renaissance. Redefine black culture. Another club called Hamilton Lodge would be known for being one of the firsts to create ballroom culture.
